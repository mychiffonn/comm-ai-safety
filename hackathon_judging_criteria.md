# Hackathon judging criteria

Projects are scored out of 100 points across five dimensions.

| Dimension | Points |
|---|---:|
| Valuable contribution to AI safety | 40 |
| Practicality / feasibility | 20 |
| Accuracy and epistemic quality | 15 |
| Novelty and creativity | 10 |
| Presentation | 10 |
| **Total** | **100** |

## 1. Valuable contribution to AI safety (40 points)

### Real gap, impact, and theory of change (15 points)

Does the project address a real, identifiable gap with clear impact and a tractable theory of change?

| Score | What it looks like |
|---:|---|
| 0–3 | No identifiable gap; impact is vague or assumed; no theory of change. |
| 4–7 | A gap is described but poorly evidenced; impact is asserted; theory of change is generic (for example, “raise awareness”). |
| 8–11 | A real gap is identified with some evidence; impact is plausible; theory of change exists but lacks specificity about steps or stakeholders. |
| 12–15 | A specific, well-evidenced gap; clear, compelling impact; and a concrete theory of change with identifiable steps, stakeholders, and a plausible path to effect. |

### Clear audience and value (10 points)

Does the project have a clear audience that it delivers value to?

| Score | What it looks like |
|---:|---|
| 0–2 | No audience, or an impossibly broad audience (for example, “everyone” or “society”). |
| 3–5 | Audience is named but general; its value is mostly assumed. |
| 6–8 | Clear, specific audience; it is evident why the project is useful to them. |
| 9–10 | Precisely defined audience; the project is tailored to its context, needs, and existing knowledge. |

### Depth or breadth (15 points)

Depth means a substantive, specific contribution to a well-defined problem. Breadth means a scalable, adaptable contribution for different audiences or workstreams.

| Score | What it looks like |
|---:|---|
| 0–3 | Neither deep nor broad; surface-level and noncommittal. |
| 4–7 | Some depth or breadth, but not convincingly either; the contribution feels incomplete or generic. |
| 8–11 | Clear depth or clear breadth; adaptable with little modification, or a meaningful contribution to a specific problem. |
| 12–15 | Excellent to exceptional depth or breadth; substantial enough to stand alone in a specific area, or immediately adoptable across multiple contexts. |

## 2. Practicality / feasibility (20 points)

Could someone outside the team do something useful with the project output? This may be a finished artifact, structured plan, framework, or other output that a third party can act on.

| Score | What it looks like |
|---:|---|
| 0–3 | Nothing usable; output is vague, incomplete, or adds noise. |
| 4–7 | Something exists but is mainly meaningful to the team; substantial support or effort is needed. |
| 8–12 | Usable with modest effort; an external person can pick it up without much context. |
| 13–17 | Clearly actionable; a specific person or organisation could implement or use it today with minimal extra work. |
| 18–20 | Immediately deployable, polished, and self-contained; a third party can use, publish, or act on it without consulting the team. |

## 3. Accuracy and epistemic quality (15 points)

### Accurate representation of AI safety (8 points)

Does the project accurately reflect the state of AI safety and its issues?

| Score | What it looks like |
|---:|---|
| 0–1 | Significant factual errors or meaningful misrepresentation of AI-safety concepts, risks, or the field. |
| 2–4 | Mostly accurate, with notable gaps, oversimplifications, or outdated framing. |
| 5–7 | Accurate and well-informed, reflecting current field understanding without significant errors. |
| 8 | Exceptional accuracy, including genuine familiarity with nuances, contestation, and uncertainty. |

### Verifiable assumptions and claims (7 points)

Can the project’s assumptions and claims be easily verified?

| Score | What it looks like |
|---:|---|
| 0–1 | Unsupported claims; key assumptions are hidden, unstated, or untested. |
| 2–4 | Some support; assumptions are acknowledged but not fully examined. |
| 5–6 | Most claims are verifiable; assumptions and limits are explicit. |
| 7 | All major claims are well sourced; assumptions are explicit and defensible, with contested points acknowledged. |

## 4. Novelty and creativity (10 points)

Does the project bring something new through the problem it addresses, the way it approaches it, or both?

| Score | What it looks like |
|---:|---|
| 0–1 | Neither problem nor approach is novel; adds little to known work. |
| 2–4 | Some novelty in one dimension: a slightly fresh angle or a somewhat underexplored problem. |
| 5–7 | Clear novelty in one dimension: an underexplored gap is filled, or a familiar problem is approached unconventionally. |
| 8–9 | Strong novelty in one dimension and some in the other, or exceptional novelty in one; memorable and field-relevant. |
| 10 | Novel in both problem and approach; surprising yet highly relevant. |

## 5. Presentation (10 points)

Presenting is optional but encouraged. Presentations will likely last three to five minutes and need not use slides.

### Clarity (5 points)

| Score | What it looks like |
|---:|---|
| 0–1 | Hard to follow; project, audience, or purpose is unclear. |
| 2–3 | Mostly clear, but key elements require explanation or prompting. |
| 4 | Clear throughout; the audience understands the project and its value unaided. |
| 5 | Exceptionally clear; the project communicates itself without ambiguity. |

### Engagement (5 points)

| Score | What it looks like |
|---:|---|
| 0–1 | Flat or disengaging. |
| 2–3 | Competent but not memorable. |
| 4 | Engaging; sustains attention and generates genuine interest. |
| 5 | Compelling; generates enthusiasm or curiosity and makes the audience want to learn more or use it. |
